An Act to establish the of f ice and functions of Coroners in the Cook Islands and to provide
a public procedure for the investigation by a public of f icer of violent, unexplained or
suspicious deaths.
BE IT ENACTED by the Legislative Assembly of the Cook Islands in Session
assembled, and by the authority of the same as follows:
1 Short title
This Act may be cited as the Coroners Act 1979–80.
2 Appointment of coroners
(1) The Queen’s Representative may upon the recommendation of the Minister of
Justice from time to time by warrant under his or her hand appoint ﬁt persons to
be coroners to exercise jurisdiction throughout the Cook Islands.
(2) Every coroner shall hold of f ice for 3 years from the date of appointment and is
eligible for reappointment.
(3) Every coroner must retire from of f ice on attaining the age of 70 years.
(4) Every coroner may at any time resign his or her of f ice by notice in writing to the
Minister of Justice.
(5) No person is eligible to hold the of f ice of coroner unless—
(a) he or she is a barrister and solicitor of the High Court of New Zealand of
5 years standing or more; or
(b) he or she is a Commissioner of the High Court who has served a term of
not less than 12 months immediately preceding appointment; or
(c) he or she is the Registrar of the High Court of the Cook Islands.

3 Crown pathologists
(1) The Minister of Justice may from time to time appoint any medical practitioner
(being a medical practitioner registered to practice either in the Cook Islands or
in New Zealand) to be a Crown pathologist.
(2) A Crown pathologist may be appointed from private or public practice.
(3) A coroner may if he or she considers it necessary or desirable appoint a Crown
pathologist to conduct or attend any post mortem examination or to make any
analysis or report under section 10(4) or otherwise to assist the investigation
into any death in respect of which an inquest is required to be held under this
Act.
Functions and powers of coroners
4 Functions of coroners
(1) The principal functions of a coroner are to inquire, in accordance with the
provisions of this Act, into the manner of death of any person in any case where
this Act requires that the death be reported to a coroner.

(2) Every coroner has, for the purpose of exercising any of the powers, functions,
or duties conferred or imposed on him or her by this Act, the same powers,
privileges, authorities, and immunities as are possessed by any judicial of f icer
exercising his or her jurisdiction under any enactment of the Cook Islands and
in particular has power to issue summonses for and warrants to enforce the
attendance of witnesses at an inquest or inquiry under this Act, to maintain order
at any such inquest or inquiry , to administer oaths, to examine witnesses, to
punish for contempt, and to adjourn any proceedings before him or her from
time to time and from place to place.
5 When coroner to hold inquest
(1) A coroner must hold an inquest in each case either on the coroner’s own
initiative or where he or she is informed that a person is dead and there is
reasonable cause to suspect that the person—
(a) has died either a violent or an unnatural death; or
(b) has died while in the legal custody of the Superintendent of a penal
institution; or
(c) had died in such a place or under such circumstances that, in accordance
with the provisions of any enactment other than this Act, an inquest is
required to be held.
(2) A coroner must hold an inquest where he or she is informed that any person has
died a sudden death of which the case is unknown:
Provided that in any such case the coroner may decide, in accordance with
section 6, not to hold an inquest.
(3) It is the duty of any person ﬁnding a person lying dead, or having knowledge of
the death of any person in any of the circumstances set forth in the foregoing
provisions of this section, to report the death to a constable, who must thereupon
report the death to the coroner.
(4) After a constable has reported the death to a coroner in accordance with
subsection (3), the police must make such inquiries as may be necessary for the
purposes of this Act or as may be directed by the coroner.
6 Inquest need not be held in certain circumstances
(1) Where any sudden death of which the cause is unknown is reported to a coroner
and he or she is of the opinion that further inquiries or a post mortem
examination may prove an inquest to be unnecessary , the coroner may direct any
inquiries he or she thinks proper to be made and may authorise any registered
medical practitioner or Crown pathologist to hold a post mortem examination of
the body and to report the result thereof to him or her in writing.
(2) Where the coroner, as a result of the post mortem examination or of inquiries
made by him or her, is satisﬁed that the death was due to natural causes and did
not take place in such place or in such circumstances as to necessitate the
holding of an inquest in accordance with the requirements of any enactment, the
coroner may decide not to hold an inquest or may discontinue any inquest which
he or she has begun.
(3) If, in accordance with this section, a coroner decides not to hold an inquest or
to discontinue an inquest the coroner must, in the prescribed form, notify the
Secretary for Justice of his or her decision.

7 Powers of Judge or Commissioner when no coroner available
(1) Where a death occurs in such circumstances that the death should be reported to
a coroner in accordance with the provisions of section 5 and no coroner is
available to act, the death may be reported by a constable to a Judge or
Commissioner of the High Court who has thereupon in respect of the death the
following powers and functions—
(a) to authorise a post mortem examination and generally to exercise the
powers and functions of a coroner under section 10:
(b) to open an inquest:
(c) to hear and record evidence of identiﬁcation:
(d) to authorise the burial of the body:
(e) to adjourn any inquest open by him or her under this section.
(2) Where an inquest has been opened by a Judge or Commissioner under this
section, the inquest must be completed by a coroner in all respects as if the death
had been originally reported to him or her:
Provided that the coroner may act upon any evidence already given before the
Judge or Commissioner in all respects as if it were given before the coroner.
8 Inquest where body destroyed or irrecoverable
Where a coroner has reason to believe that a death has occurred in such
circumstances that an inquest ought to be held and, because of the provisions of
section 15, an inquest cannot be held except by virtue of the provisions of this
section, the coroner may report the facts to the Minister of Justice and the
Minister of Justice may , if he or she considers it desirable so to do, direct an
inquest to be held in respect of the death, and an inquest must be held
accordingly by the coroner making the report or such other coroner as the
Minister of Justice may direct, and the provisions of this Act apply , with the
necessary modiﬁcations, to any such inquest.
9 Coroner may require medical report
Whether or not an inquest is being held, the coroner may require any medical
practitioner who has recently attended the person into whose death the coroner
is inquiring to supply him or her with a report relating to the deceased person.
10 Coroner may authorise post mortem examination
(1) The coroner may at any time before the termination of the inquest authorise any
registered medical practitioner or Crown pathologist other than a medical
practitioner who, to the knowledge of the coroner, has attended the deceased
person immediately prior to his or her death, to perform a post mortem
examination of the body of the deceased person.
(2) Any medical practitioner who has attended the deceased person immediately
prior to his or her death is entitled to be present at the examination. The coroner
may , if he or she thinks necessary , have notice served on any such medical
practitioner requiring him or her to attend the post mortem examination or to
submit a report for the assistance of the person holding the post mortem
examination.
(3) Upon application being made to him or her, the coroner may permit any other
person to be represented by a medical practitioner as an observer at any such
post mortem examination.

Inquest
12 Purpose of inquest
An inquest shall be conducted by the coroner for the purpose of establishing—
(a) the fact that a person had died:
(b) the identity of the deceased person:
(c) when, where, and how the death occurred.
13 Inquest to be before coroner alone
(1) All inquests must be held before a coroner alone.
(2) Any inquest may , at the direction of the coroner, be held concurrently with any
other inquiry or judicial proceedings.
14 Date and place of inquest
Where an inquest is to be held the coroner must ﬁx the date, time, and place of
the inquest, and give notice thereof to the senior of f icer of police in the place
where the inquest is to be held, who must give notice of the date, time, and place
of the inquest to such persons as he or she thinks ﬁt, or as the coroner directs.
15 View of body
(1) Subject to the provisions of section 8, no inquest may be concluded unless the
coroner is satisﬁed that the body in respect of which the inquest is being held
has been viewed by some person giving evidence at the inquest.
(2) Where an inquest or any inquiry under this Act is held by a coroner it is not be
necessary for the coroner to view the body in respect of which the inquest or
inquiry is held.
16 Hearing in public
(1) Subject to the provisions of this Act, the room in which an inquest is held must
be open to the public:
Provided that if the coroner considers it desirable in the interests of justice,
decency , or order, he or she may exclude all or any persons from the whole or
any part of the proceedings at the inquest or may prohibit the publication of any
part of the evidence given at the inquest.
(2) The coroner may direct that any witness not yet heard at an inquest must go and
remain outside the place of hearing until required to give evidence.
17 Coroner to examine witnesses on oath
(1) The coroner must at the inquest examine on oath all persons who tender their
evidence respecting the facts in issue and all other persons whom he or she
thinks it expedient to examine.
(2) At any inquest any person who, in the opinion of the coroner, has a suf f icient
interest in the subject or result of the inquest may attend personally or by
counsel, and may examine and cross-examine witnesses.
(3) In all proceedings under this Act the coroner may admit any evidence that he or
she thinks ﬁt, whether or not the same is otherwise admissible in a court of law ,
but no evidence may be admitted by the coroner for the purposes of the inquest
unless in his or her opinion the evidence is necessary for the purpose of
establishing any of the matters referred to in section 12.

(4) Where—
(a) the coroner is satisﬁed that there is no reason making it desirable that the
witness should give the whole of their evidence orally; and
(b) no person attending the inquest and entitled to examine and
cross-examine witnesses objects to the procedure being followed,
the coroner may permit any witness to give the whole or part of their evidence
by tendering a previously prepared statement in writing and conﬁrming the
same on oath. The witness may be cross-examined on any evidence in the
statement as if it had been given orally at the inquest, and the statement forms
part of the depositions taken at the inquest.
(5) The coroner must put into writing the evidence admitted by him or her at the
inquest, and the depositions of each witness giving evidence at the inquest must
be read over to and signed by him or her and by the coroner.
18 Evidence at a distance
(1) Where in the opinion of the coroner it is necessary or expedient to have the
evidence of some person taken at a place other than where the coroner intends
to hold or is holding an inquest, the coroner may , by writing under his or her
hand, appoint some other coroner or, if no coroner is available, a Commissioner
or Chief Administration Of f icer to take the evidence of that person.
(2) The coroner or Commissioner or Chief Administration Of f icer so appointed has,
for the purpose of any such examination, the same powers under this Act as if
they were conducting the inquest, and must appoint a time and place for taking
the evidence and cause notice to be given to any persons in accordance with the
direction of the coroner holding the inquest.
(3) Any persons notiﬁed in accordance with subsection (2) and any other person so
permitted by the coroner or Commissioner or Chief Administration Of f icer
taking evidence may attend personally or by counsel, and may examine and
cross-examine witnesses in the same manner and to the same extent as if the
proceedings were an inquest.
(4) The evidence of any witness so examined must be reduced to writing, read over
to the witness and signed by him or her and by the coroner or Commissioner or
Chief Administration Of f icer before whom it is taken, and forwarded by him or
her to the coroner holding the inquest, who may receive and act upon any such
evidence in all respects as if it had been given before him or her at the inquest.
19 Witness neglecting to attend
Every witness on whom any summons is served, either personally or by leaving
the same at his or her residence with an occupant appearing to be above the age
of 14 years, in suf f icient time for the witness to obey the same, who fails without
good suf f icient excuse to obey any such summons, and every medical
practitioner who is directed by a coroner to attend a post mortem examination or
to furnish a report in accordance with this Act, and who neglects or refuses to
obey any such summons, direction, or order, commits an of fence and is liable on
summary conviction to a ﬁne not exceeding $40.

21 Inquest where death was self inﬂicted
(1) Subject to the provisions of this Act, where it appears to the coroner at the
commencement or in the course of an inquest that the circumstances are such
that it appears possible that death may have been self inﬂicted, the coroner may
direct that no report, or no further report of the proceedings be published until
after he or she has made his or her ﬁnding.
(2) Where the coroner ﬁnds that the death was self inﬂicted, no report of the
proceedings of the inquest may , without the authority of the coroner, be
published other than the name, address, and occupation of the deceased person,
the fact that an inquest has been held, and that the coroner has found that the
death was self inﬂicted.

(2) The Judge may , in their absolute discretion, and on such grounds as they think

any decision given by the Judge is ﬁnal and binding on all persons.
(3) Where an application is made to a Judge under this section in respect of an order
or direction of a coroner, the order or direction remains in full force and ef fect
until the decision of the Judge in respect of the application is made.

and a certiﬁcate of the registration of the death, must be transmitted by the
coroner to the Secretary for Justice.

Deaths in Outer Islands
25 Special provisions for deaths in Outer Islands
(1) When a death occurs in any of the Outer Islands in such circumstances that the
provisions of section 5 apply and there is no coroner or person authorised by
section 7 to open an inquest then the police constable to whom the death is
reported must require a medical of f icer or other qualiﬁed medical practitioner or
medical personnel to either—
(a) perform a post mortem either with or without instruction from a Crown
pathologist; or
(b) conduct a medical inspection of the body either with or without
instruction from a Crown pathologist; or
(c) compile a report relating to the deceased either with or without
instruction from a Crown pathologist.
(2) For the purposes of subsection (1) a Crown pathologist may give instructions by
radio telephone or other means of communication.
(3) No burial may be authorised or take place where death has occurred in
circumstances that the provisions of subsection (1) apply until such time as the
provisions of subsection (1) have been carried out.
(4) Any police constable to whom a death is reported under subsection (1) must as
soon as practicable after receipt of information as to the death advise the nearest
coroner, Judge or Commissioner who may authorise burial and open and
complete an inquest in accordance with the provisions of this Act with any
necessary modiﬁcations.
(5) Any police constable to whom a death is reported under subsection (1) may
authorise burial of the body after the medical examination required has been
concluded in the absence of authorisation from a coroner, Judge or
Commissioner if—
(a) after reasonable attempts have been made it is not possible to notify a
coroner, Judge, or Commissioner; and
(b) the condition of the body or the interests of public health require
immediate burial.
(6) Where a burial is authorised by a constable under subsection (5) the constable
must lodge a full report on the death and circumstances surrounding burial with
the nearest coroner. Such report may be lodged by radio telephone, telegram, or
other means of communication.

It is the duty of the police to assist at all inquests, inquiries, and investigations
under this Act.

28 Inquest may be reopened
(1) Where an inquest has been concluded and it is shown to the satisfaction of the

the coroner may accept such of the ﬁndings and of the evidence given at the

(3) At the conclusion of the reopened inquest the coroner must give his or her
ﬁndings in the manner provided by section 24 and any such ﬁnding replaces for
all purposes the ﬁnding previously given.
29 Protection of witnesses and counsel
Every witness attending and giving evidence at any inquest or inquiry held
under this Act and every counsel appearing before a coroner has the same
privileges and immunities as witnesses and counsel in courts of law .

31 Inquests may be held on Sundays
It is lawful to hold any inquest upon a Sunday whenever in the opinion of the
coroner it is expedient to hold the same on that day .

Pursuant to Article 7(2) of the Constitution and the Coroners Act 1979, the Chief Justice
of the High Court, acting by and with the advice and consent of the Executive Council,
hereby makes the following regulations:
1 Short title and commencement
(1) These regulations may be cited as the Coroners Regulations 1979.
(2) These regulations come into force on the day after the date of their notiﬁcation
in the Cook Islands Gazette.
2 Interpretation
In these regulations unless the context otherwise requires—
the Act means the Coroners Act 1979
coroner means a coroner appointed under the Coroners Act 1979 and includes
any person authorised to open or conduct an inquest under the provisions of that
Act
Minister means the Minister of Justice
Secretary means the Secretary for Justice.
3 Inspection of documents
(1) There is payable in respect of any inspection of any documents relating to
proceedings before a coroner a fee of $0.50, and if a copy of any such
documents or part thereof is supplied a fee at the rate of $0.20 a page is payable.
(2) Despite the foregoing provisions of this regulation, the Secretary , may in his or
her discretion dispense with the payment of any fee under this regulation.
4 Authority for post mortem
Where in accordance with the Act a coroner authorises a post mortem
examination he or she may give the authority in the Form No 1 in the Schedule.
5 Inquest discontinued
(1) Where pursuant to section 6 a coroner decides not to hold or to discontinue an
inquest in respect of any death, he or she must notify the Secretary in the Form
No 2 in the Schedule, and shall forward with the notiﬁcation the report of any
post mortem examination or any other written report of a medical practitioner
supplies to the coroner in respect of the death.
(2) A copy of the notiﬁcation referred to in subclause (1) must be forwarded by the
coroner to the Registrar of Births and Deaths who is required to register the
death.
6 Order for burial
Where in accordance with the Act a coroner gives an order for the burial of the
body of any person whose death has been reported to him or her, the order shall
be in the Form No 3 in the Schedule and a copy of the order must be forwarded
to the Registrar of Births and Deaths who is required to register the death.
7 Appointment to take evidence
(1) A coroner desiring to have evidence taken at a place other than where he or she
intends to hold or is holding an inquest must forward to the coroner who is to

take the evidence an appointment in the Form No 4 in the Schedule, together
with any documents or exhibits which the coroner holding the inquest considers
should be available when the examination of witnesses is held.
(2) The coroner holding the inquest must forward a copy of the appointment to the
police.
(3) All depositions taken by the coroner taking any such evidence, together with
any exhibits produced at the taking of the evidence and any documents or
exhibits sent by the coroner holding the inquest, must, when the evidence has
been taken, be forwarded to the coroner holding the inquest.
8 Summons to witness
(1) Where in accordance with the Act a coroner desires any person to give evidence
he or she may cause to be served on that person a summons in the Form No 5
in the Schedule.
(2) The oath administered to any person giving evidence under the Act before a
coroner may be in the Form No 6 in the Schedule.
9 Deposition of witnesses
On the completion of an inquest a coroner must notify the Secretary in the Form
No 7 in the Schedule of all depositions under the Act taken by a coroner and
shall on the same form sign his or her ﬁndings in respect of the inquest and shall
forward the form together with the depositions and, where applicable, a
certiﬁcate of registration of the death to the Secretary .
10 Inquest not resumed
Where pursuant to section 20 a coroner decides that an inquest will not be
resumed he or she must notify the Secretary in the Form No 8 in the Schedule,
and shall forward with the notiﬁcation any depositions taken at the inquest and
a certiﬁcate of registration of the death in respect of which the inquest was
opened.
11 Coroners fees
(1) These are payable to such coroners not being Judges, Commissioners or the
Registrar of the High Court, as the Minister may direct salaries at such rates as
may from time to time be ﬁxed by the Minister with the concurrence of the
Minister of Finance.
(2) There is payable to every coroner, other than a Judge, Commissioner, Registrar
of the High Court or a coroner who is in receipt of a salary under subclause (1),
the following fees, that is to say—
(a) for every inquest held by him or her, a fee of $22.50:
(b) where 2 or more persons are killed on the one occasion and the
circumstances of their death are investigated by him or her at a single
inquest or at several inquests on the same day or on consecutive working
days in respect of the ﬁrst person a fee of $22.50 and in respect of every
other such person a fee of $8.50:
(c) for every case where he or she decides under sections of the Act not to
hold or to discontinue an inquest, a fee of $11.25.
(3) Where any coroner to whom subclause (2) applies takes evidence on behalf of
another coroner, there is in respect of time spent when travelling in the
performance of his or her duties as a coroner, fees at the rates from time to time
ﬁxed in that behalf.

12 Medical practitioners’ fees
(1) Where in accordance with the Act a medical practitioner performs a post
mortem examination at the request of a coroner there is payable to the
practitioner a fee of $30, which sum is deemed to include payment for any
conferences with the coroner or the police:
Provided also that, where any other medical practitioner, whether in the course
of giving evidence or otherwise, provides a detailed report of the examination
there may be paid for the report a further fee of $20.
(2) Where a medical practitioner who has performed a post mortem examination
under the Act gives evidence at an inquest, he or she must be paid an additional
fee equal in amount to the fee for the time being payable to an expert witness
under the Code of Civil Procedure of the High Court.
(3) Where a pathologist (including a Crown pathologist) examines specimens at the
request of a medical practitioner who is performing a post mortem under the
Act, the pathologist may be paid a fee for that work to be determined in the
same manner as the qualifying fee for the time being prescribed for an expert
witness by the Code of Civil Procedure of the High Court.
(4) Where a medical practitioner or Crown pathologist at the request of a coroner
makes an examination which does not amount to a post mortem examination, or
makes a report, whether or not a recent examination has been made by him or
her, there must be paid to him or her a fee of $4.
(5) A medical practitioner who performs any function under the Act for which no
fee is otherwise provided may be paid such fee, not exceeding $8, as the
Secretary , in his or her discretion, thinks ﬁt.
13 Witnesses’ fees and allowances
Except as provided by regulation 12, any witness (including a medical witness)
and any interpreter who attends at the instance of the police to give evidence at
an inquest or to whom the coroner authorises payment may be paid fees,
allowances, and expenses in accordance with the Code of Civil Procedure of the
High Court, and the provisions of those regulations apply accordingly as if
references therein to the court were references to a coroner.
14 Fees in exceptional circumstances
The Secretary , in any case where he or she considers that exceptional
circumstances render any such course advisable, may authorise the payment of
a fee or allowance greater than that prescribed by these regulations.
